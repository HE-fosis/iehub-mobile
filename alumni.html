ISoc Alumni Development Guides

Select Your ISoc Stage

Every ISoc is at a different point in their alumni journey. Select the stage that best describes your society to view your tailored roadmap.

Many ISocs do not have an established alumni network, and that’s completely normal. What matters most is taking simple, intentional steps to reconnect with past members and create a foundation future committees can build upon.

1 Identify the Alumni Landscape

Before reaching out, understand who your alumni are and where they might be. Build a basic picture, even if the list starts small.

  • Create a simple database (Google Sheets/Notion) of previous committee members.
  • Search for former members on LinkedIn who reference the university.
  • Ask current and older students for contacts—word of mouth is highly effective.
Aim for 5-10 solid contacts to start.

2 Make Initial Contact

The first message sets the tone. Keep it short, simple, and welcoming without applying pressure.

  • Draft a personalised message thanking them for their past contribution.
  • Create a simple online form for them to register interest and state how they’d like to be involved (updates, mentoring, etc.). Ensure you include a GDPR data consent statement.
  • Set up a stall at graduation events to catch people in person.

3 Build the Alumni Group

Once alumni respond, focus on connection rather than heavy structure. Give them a network too!

  • Create a central communication channel (e.g., a WhatsApp group for smaller numbers).
  • Nominate one or two "Alumni Liaisons" from the ISoc committee to maintain contact.
  • Organise an informal first gathering to rebuild familiarity and trust.

4 Establish Purpose and Light Structure

Alumni involvement works best when expectations are clear but flexible. Consistency matters more than intensity.

  • Ask alumni what they want from the relationship (social, spiritual, professional networking).
  • Identify shared goals (mentorship, sustaining the ISoc long-term).
  • Propose a loose structure like occasional mentoring sessions or termly socials.

5 Sustain and Grow the Network

The long-term goal is continuity. Each year should strengthen the network rather than starting from zero.

  • Share regular, short newsletters highlighting ISoc activities.
  • Invite them to annual dinners, Ramadan iftars, and major conferences.
  • Document contacts, processes, and lessons learned for a smooth handover to the next committee.

You already have some contact with old members. Now is the time to consolidate those connections and turn individual contacts into a structured, connected group.

1 Consolidate Contact Information

Bring all separate group chats and contacts into one central list to establish a true foundation.

  • Check if any informal alumni groups already exist locally.
  • Leverage current connections by asking your committee for access to previous years' group chats.
  • Engage with the university alumni team to obtain contacts for old committees if possible.

2 Appoint an Alumni Liaison

Dedicated communication is key to maintaining the relationship.

  • Choose someone dedicated (ideally without a heavy existing committee role) to fully focus on the network.
  • Keep communications exclusively through this liaison so alumni associate them with important updates.

3 Create Structured Communication

Turn individuals into a connected community by giving them a dedicated space.

  • Send a recruitment message to your consolidated list stating the clear purpose of the network.
  • Create a WhatsApp Community with sub-groups (e.g., Brothers, Sisters, Medicine, Engineering).
  • Use an Announcements channel for updates so they don't get buried in normal chat.
  • Maintain a mailing list for those who prefer email over WhatsApp.

4 Invite Alumni Back

Integrate them back into the campus ecosystem.

  • Ensure other Muslim societies (Muslim Medics, etc.) are aware of the network for speaker events or mentorship programmes.
  • Invite alumni to ISoc dinners, treks, and key socials.
Commitment is optional; consistency is key.

5 Sustain with Newsletters & Quick Wins

Keep the momentum going with regular, low-pressure touchpoints.

  • Send a quarterly newsletter highlighting "Quick Wins" and ISoc achievements.
  • Subtly include donation links for those who wish to support financially.
  • Ensure a proper handover of all databases to the next committee.

Recognising a lack of engagement is usually due to lost contact, not a lack of interest. This stage requires a warm, patient approach to rebuild trust and relationships.

1 Audit Old Chats & Archives

Start digging to build a basic picture of who your alumni are.

  • Try to gain access to old WhatsApp chats or mailing lists.
  • Recruit a dedicated team to help review social media archives.
  • Review past alumni events to see what worked well historically and what didn't.

2 Send a Personalised Warm Message

Reach out with a focus on reconnection, not requests.

  • Draft a message acknowledging the gap in communication and expressing a sincere desire to rebuild ties.
  • Ensure they feel welcomed back without feeling obligated to commit time or money immediately.

3 Organise a "Reconnection Event"

Focus purely on relationship building and familiarity.

  • Host an informal alumni mingle with food and games to give them a reason to come.
  • Consider relaxed environments like a cricket match watch party or a casual dinner.

4 Document Everything

Restarting a network is hard work; make sure it isn't lost again.

  • Keep track of what communication styles and events worked, and which fell flat.
  • Create a robust handover document for future committees so they can pick up exactly where you left off.

5 Low-Pressure Mailing List

Allow them to observe the ISoc's progress from a distance until they are ready to engage more deeply.

  • Add interested individuals to a low-pressure mailing list for future updates.
  • Send short termly updates highlighting ISoc activities.
  • Slowly begin inviting them to major events like annual dinners or graduations.

For established ISocs, maintaining and growing an alumni network is just as important as starting one, but it requires a more formal structure and professional execution.

1 Create an Alumni Executive Group

Unless dedicated roles exist, alumni work will fall to the bottom of the priority list.

  • Recruit a dedicated team strictly to maintain and grow the alumni network.
  • Ensure you have a method to build the database year-on-year (e.g., graduation forms pushed to all Muslim students).

2 Formalise Roles & Vision

Split the workload efficiently based on the network's goals.

  • Establish a clear vision for the year (events, newsletters, fundraising).
  • Create specific roles: Mentorship Lead, Events Lead, Finance/Fundraising Lead.

3 Host a High-Level Annual Dinner

Provide a premier event for alumni to reconnect and contribute.

  • Sort a venue months in advance; alumni require more notice to make arrangements than current students.
  • Keep the focus on alumni. Initially, avoid opening it to all students, though final-year students can attend as future alumni.
  • Subtly fundraise by having QR codes for donations on tables.
  • Use committee alumni to promote the event within their old university circles.

4 Launch Structured Career Networking

Provide tangible, professional value to the community.

  • Establish a strong LinkedIn presence to find alumni and promote mentorship opportunities.
  • Pair alumni with current students studying the same course or entering the same profession.
  • Maintain a regular, professional newsletter.

5 Connect to the National FOSIS Body

Expand the horizons of your local network.

  • Reach out to FOSIS to link your individual alumni network to the national body.
  • Invite alumni to act as Khateebs or speakers for major campaigns (like Charity Week or DIW), as they relate well to the current student experience.
Link up with FOSIS for national impact.
